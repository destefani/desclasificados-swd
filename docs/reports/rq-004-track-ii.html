US Government Actions to Prevent Allende's Election (1964-1970)

Track I and Track II Operations from Declassified CIA Documents

Research Question

What did the US government do to prevent Salvador Allende from being elected president of Chile?

This report examines declassified CIA documents revealing US covert operations to prevent Allende's election, including propaganda campaigns, funding to opposition parties, and the secret Track II operation initiated by President Nixon.

1. Track I: Covert Political Action (1964-1970)

The US government conducted extensive covert operations through the 40 Committee (the interagency group that authorized covert actions) to influence Chilean elections:

1964 Presidential Election

Document 24828 (UNCLASSIFIED, 1969) details prior CIA operations:

"U.S. had already engaged in covert operations to support candidates against Allende, including financial assistance to President Frei during the 1964 elections and ongoing support to selected Congressional candidates."

The CIA provided approximately $3 million to Eduardo Frei's 1964 campaign - one of the largest covert electoral interventions in CIA history at the time.

Source: Doc 24828, CIA Operations Report

1970 Presidential Election

Document 24916 (UNCLASSIFIED, October 1970) outlines planned actions:

"The courses of action recommended are posited on the following assumptions: Salvador Allende will be elected October 24th by the Chilean Congress..."

This memorandum details CIA plans to provide financial support to anti-Allende forces, including funding for radio stations, newspapers, and direct assistance to opposition parties.

Source: Doc 24916, October 16, 1970 Memorandum

2. Propaganda Campaign

Document 24935 (UNCLASSIFIED, August 1970) from Charles A. Meyer outlines propaganda options:

"Limited actions such as a propaganda campaign to sway the congressional vote if Allende was a close contender... a propaganda campaign as a secure method to influence public opinion and political outcomes in Chile, which would be less risky than more direct actions."

The propaganda campaign was considered the safest approach because it could influence public opinion while maintaining plausible deniability. This included funding to El Mercurio newspaper and other media outlets.

Source: Doc 24935, August 31, 1970 Memorandum by Charles A. Meyer

3. Track II: Nixon's Secret Coup Order

After Allende won a plurality in the September 4, 1970 election, President Nixon initiated a secret parallel operation that bypassed normal policy channels:

Document 24885 (SECRET, August 1975) by William G. Hyland explicitly references Track II:

"A covert operation known as 'Track II' was initiated following President Nixon's instructions on September 15, 1970, to prevent Allende from taking power. This operation was not discussed with the 40 Committee and involved clandestine efforts to undermine Allende's presidency."

Key characteristics of Track II:

  • Bypassed normal channels - Hidden from the 40 Committee, State Department, and US Ambassador to Chile
  • Direct presidential order - Nixon instructed CIA Director Richard Helms on September 15, 1970
  • Military coup objective - Aimed to promote a military coup before the October 24 Congressional vote
  • Weapons transfers - Involved passing weapons to Chilean conspirators

Source: Doc 24885, SECRET Memorandum by William G. Hyland

This confirms that CIA official William Broe was actively exploring options to prevent Allende from taking office before the Congressional confirmation vote.

Source: Doc 24776, Report dated May 8, 1976

4. The "Alessandri Gambit"

Document 24844 references a constitutional scheme to prevent Allende's confirmation:

"USG officials encourage, support, or suggest to Alessandri and/or Frei supporters that they put into operation a plan which would have Alessandri elected by the Chilean Congress on October 24 followed..."

The plan: Congress would elect runner-up Jorge Alessandri instead of Allende. Alessandri would then immediately resign, triggering new elections where incumbent Eduardo Frei (constitutionally barred from immediate re-election) could run again.

Source: Doc 24844, Memorandum

5. Official Denials and Gaps

Document 24884 (SECRET, October 1981) compiles embassy responses to Senate inquiries:

"Many officials claimed they did not recall specific instructions from Nixon regarding promoting a coup or direct interference in the electoral process."

Document 24842 documents the official public stance:

"Government and the Embassy at Santiago were very careful to avoid becoming involved in Chilean politics in order to avoid any charges of intervention in the internal affairs of Chile."

This reveals the deliberate disconnect between public denials and covert actions.

Conclusions

What the Documents Prove

  • Electoral intervention was systematic - The US funded opposition candidates, media outlets, and political parties across multiple Chilean elections from 1964-1970.
  • Track II was a secret coup operation - President Nixon ordered the CIA to prevent Allende from taking power through any means, bypassing normal policy channels and hiding the operation from the State Department and US Ambassador.
  • Weapons were transferred - Documents confirm that weapons were passed to Chilean conspirators as part of Track II.
  • Constitutional schemes were explored - The US actively promoted the "Alessandri gambit" to manipulate the Congressional confirmation vote.
  • Public denials were deliberate deception - While publicly claiming non-interference, the US conducted extensive covert operations.

Historical Context

The declassified documents reveal that US intervention in Chilean elections was not limited to 1970, but was part of a sustained campaign dating back to at least 1964. The distinction between Track I (approved covert action) and Track II (secret presidential directive) became central to the Church Committee investigations in 1975-1976.

Track II ultimately failed to prevent Allende's confirmation on October 24, 1970. However, it set the stage for continued US hostility toward the Allende government, including economic pressure and support for opposition forces that culminated in the September 11, 1973 military coup.

Limitations

Many documents remain partially redacted or classified. Some officials' testimonies contain claims of not recalling specific events, and the full scope of Track II operations may not be fully documented in available records.
